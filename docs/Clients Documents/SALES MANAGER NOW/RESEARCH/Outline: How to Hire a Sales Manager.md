#### **The Philosophy of a Sales Manager**

**Who is a Sales Manager?**

In a growing small to medium-sized business, a sales manager can see the team structure, strategy, and process needed to meet the company's assigned sales and revenue goals. They then guide and develop a team to trust and follow the process to stretch and attain or exceed the goals.

Over the decades, one family company trusted family members and proven industry sales stars to manage its sales team. It led to a successful small business, but not what they had hoped for. They struggled with growing past 4 million in sales. They hired a sales manager who focused more on defining clear expectations, creating an environment where salespeople were held accountable for their activity and sales results. They then developed an onboarding process that used teammates as mentors to increase the team size and performance. Before long, the company was a 10 million dollar company, no family members were selling, and new long-term sales team members had arrived.

Previous managers focused on teaching others how to sell and prospect better, and motivate them to try and do more. Sometimes they did and sometimes not. What was missing was an agreed-upon process and expectations to complement the teaching. The new manager was focused on building a structure, strategy, and processes for the department, then hired and developed his people within the process to succeed. Structure (type of people you need), strategy (how you’ll go to market), and processes (what you do repeatedly) lay the groundwork to hire the Right People into the now defined, Right Seats.

The sales manager you want to hire uses their vision to inspire and their determination and grit to coach and challenge their team to perform and find a way to win. They see beyond the current people on the team and build a department that current and future team members can succeed in.

**The Player-Coach Fallback**

In most small businesses, one of the better salespeople is often “crowned” as the sales manager and paid an override or bump in salary. In actuality, they are what is known as a Player-Coach or Seller-Manager. They are responsible for carrying a sales quota and coaching the other salespeople to meet their goals. In most cases, the Player-Coach tries to teach the other salespeople how to sell similarly to how they do, but usually without process and accountability to reinforce the teaching. The Player-Coach is often not as good at teaching others as they are at selling. The skills to teach different sales personalities and to lead and manage a group are quite different than selling to customers and being solely responsible for your results.

Making this move to create a Player-Coach role seems logical. They know your business and how to sell, so they can teach others to do the same. Right? Usually wrong. The motivations to use this fallback are familiarity with the Player-Coach, confidence that they know how to sell your product/service, and it keeps you from having to hire a manager, which is cost-saving.

In my early thirties, I was in this position. I was happy to receive the override commission, but I soon discovered I had no patience or interest in teaching others sales tactics and nuances that came naturally to me. Reflecting, I was unsure how to describe principles or objectives I was working toward that drove my phrasing, pace and tempo of a conversation. I had not been schooled on developing other salespeople; I was just selling. If I could not teach them what I was good at, how would I know how to lead and manage a team? I couldn’t, and my first stint as a Player-Coach ended in about two weeks.

Depending on the size of your team, some organizations are hiring fractional sales managers to keep the top seller selling while bringing in a low-cost expert who knows how to lead, manage, and coach a team. In fact, we were the sales manager hired at the company who went from 4 million to 10 million.

**Pitfalls of Hiring the Wrong Sales Manager**

You might feel like you’ve put things off long enough, and it’s time just to hire a manager, so you do it quickly. It often happens when someone is referred to you and seems reasonable enough, especially if you do not have a job description, comp plan, or others to compare against. Slow down, you think you came across a windfall, but you will most likely hire them to get this issue off your plate. Here are some pitfalls to hiring the wrong sales manager.

**Financial Impact**

If you are not ready, a person with credentials could put the negotiation screws to you and you will over pay for someone who might leave in less than six months, if they are offered a more interesting position elsewhere. On the other hand, an underqualified person will cost you lost sales and trust from the team. You could even lose a salesperson. In six months, not much change can take place. A bad hire can cost you six months of salary ($60K - $100K).

No matter how diligent you are in hiring, some people don’t work out, but the better your process is, the better your results will be. Don’t settle, it will cost you more than waiting for the right person.

**Time Drain**

Hiring the wrong or underqualified manager can drain your time with onboarding. You might spend three to six months getting this person up to speed. If you hire the right person, they will know how to find what they need after a short orientation. No nursing them up to speed. Instead, you will deal with fewer issues, more revenue, and a growing pipeline during this early time frame.

*“How long can your business wait for results before it stings?”*

**Team Damage**

There is a ripple effect amongst the sales team when you hire the wrong manager. A weak team will be led astray, and a stronger team will stop listening and wait this bad decision out. Your team does not have to like this new manager, but they do need to respect them. If respect is missing, you’ve just made it harder for your people to get through their day, having to navigate an ineffective manager. If the manager stays too long, you can risk losing your promising reps.

*“Imagine your best rep under a weak leader—would they thrive or drift?”*

**Common Traps**

**Impatience:** Rather than setting up a good hiring process that covers all details, it's easy to act too fast in a market that is not flooded with qualified candidates.

**Promote within for the wrong reasons:** Promoting a top salesperson because they want the title, more responsibility, or more money, because you are afraid they might leave if you don’t.

**Familiarity:** Hiring a friend or current salesperson seems less risky because you know who they are but don’t know if they can manage and lead a sales team.

*“Familiarity doesn’t always equal capability, so are you on a less risky path?”*

#### **Steps to Hire the Right Sales Manager**

**The Why: Know Why You Need a Sales Manager**

We covered the wrong reasons earlier, like impatience, fear of losing someone, or trying to reward a good employee. These usually don’t work out. Other wrong reasons include, hiring someone for you to manage through or to “babysit” people.

***Reasons you should be hiring a sales manager include:***

* Let go to grow: To let go of sales management oversight, allowing you to work on other business areas.
* You are ready to get unstuck: You recognize your growth is stuck. Your efforts have not worked, and a sales leader is needed to help your sales team grow and perform better.
* Scale your sales department: Your sales department depends on one or two people, and you have not been able to attract and enlarge the sales team.
* Build value for exit strategy: Similar to scale, you need a culture and structure that can replace or add new reps easily to maintain sales momentum. The department is bigger than any one person.
* You need a sales management assistant: If you will continue to lead the sales team, you might just need an assistant sales manager to manage follow up, details, administrative tasks so you can show up and lead. *If hiring a sales management assistant, call it out and title them as Assistant Sales Manager.*

**Understand The Qualities You Need**

* **Leadership**: Have they successfully led (vision, inspiration, grit) a sales team responsible for their team's goal?
* **Management:** Have they organized a department (structure, process, systems) and created accountability for people to grow and improve?
* **Teacher/Coach**: Do they have testimonials or others that can substantiate their teaching and coaching abilities that help others to improve skills and performance?
* **Team Minded**: Have they built team success with most members meeting and exceeding their individual goals? Be cautious if their teams were successful due to a superstar.
* **Industry Experience:** Leadership and management skills often trump this category, but if you need a manager to build strategic alliances, new market strategies, or be part of the selling process, this is important.
* **Cultural Fit:** Team alignment matters. *“Will their energy lift your crew or clash?”*
* **Technical Ability:** CRM, data skills—essentials or extras? *“How tech-driven is your sales game?”*

*“Picture the ideal hire, describe what you see them doing, what others are doing around them, what is improved?” Consider speaking it out and recording your thoughts. This might help you build your qualify list.*

**Do You Need a Full-Time or Part-Time Sales Manager?**

If you need to dig deeper into this question, we’ve written an article that provides answers: “[Should you hire a full-time or part-time sales manager?](https://salesmanagernow.com/should-you-hire-a-full-time-or-part-time-sales-manager/)” Here are some quick points to consider.

* How many people will they be managing? A fractional or part-time sales manager can often manage fewer than ten salespeople.
* Can your salespeople close their business and simply need coaching, or do you need a manager to close business?
* Does the pace of your sales cycle need a full-time sales manager’s presence to deal with issues each day?
* Does your preferred budget allow you to hire the quality of full-time sales manager you need?
* Do you have a full-time role for the quality of sales manager you want? What would a full-time sales manager do if the manager is not selling, and the salespeople perform well and independently?

**Decide: Hire Externally (Full-time or Fractional), Promote Internally, Do it Yourself**

**External Full-Time: Pros** - Brings new ideas and higher level experience into the company. Already knows how to lead and manage. **Cons** - Might not want to sell long term, hire cost, role might not be challenging enough for them if your team is smaller.

**External Part-Time:** **Pros** - Brings new ideas and higher level or experience into the company. Already knows how to lead and manage, cost is less than half a full time manager. **Cons** - Does not sell to customers, pace might be slower than your culture, not onsite.

**Internal Seller/Coach: Pros** - Familiarity with person and they already know the company, cost as the handle sales and management. **Cons** - Often lacks experience to lead or manage others is risky, if no one can mentor them, could reduce their production or cost you more without the results you hoped for.

**You Manager Yourself: Pros** - Cost, you know what is happening. **Cons** - You have already maxed your skill and availability in this area, so growth most likely will stay the same, and it puts limits on your time for other areas of the business.

*“Is your team primed for an insider lift or an outside spark?”*
