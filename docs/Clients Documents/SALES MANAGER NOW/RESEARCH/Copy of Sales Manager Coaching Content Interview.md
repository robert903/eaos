Q&A

### **Q: How do you define sales management coaching compared to sales coaching or sales training?**

**A:** When you are the sales manager coaching a salesperson, you're coaching from a comprehensive or a holistic perspective. You're not just there as a fraction of the sales department to listen to the conversation and help them along. You're more in tune with each salesperson. As a sales manager, you know how much to push, how much this person can take, and what will help them win that conversation or influence it more effectively because you have a broader perspective. You have metrics that you can leverage to motivate them or tap into their desire to want to change. That's the difference: the holistic and total picture while you're teaching, training, and coaching as the sales manager.

### **Q: Why is coaching an essential part of sales management for SMBs?**

**A:** Because people need to get better. Look at professional sports; they all have position coaches, as well as other specialized coaches and the head coach, because you need people to help you improve. If you have someone who knows how to coach, they are focused on helping someone get better, not just pushing numbers and trying to motivate that way.

### **Q: What makes coaching by a sales manager more impactful than just training sales reps?**

**A:** They go together, but the coaching part should reinforce training. Many times as a manager, you're actually training and coaching at the same time. It's a combo pack. The best managers will know what was learned from a training program, a video you watch together, or a book study. There are specific takeaways that the team decides to use. After being trained, learned, and taught, they put it into practice. That's when coaching comes into play; you're coaching up what people have learned.

### **Q: What challenges do SMB owners typically face when they or their sales managers don't manage effectively?**

**A:** A lot. They often run around putting out fires. The three fundamentals we work on are believing in your people, setting clear expectations, and having an accountable environment. If you don't believe in your people, either you have a problem with your perspective, or the person isn't giving you enough results to believe in them, but you need to fix that. The first two are important for an accountable environment. Without that, when someone's not managing effectively, there is no accountability because expectations aren't clear. Or, a manager who isn't in tune with whether they believe in someone is expecting worse results and getting them.

### **Q: How do you know when your sales manager needs coaching support versus when they need full management training?**

**A:** If your sales manager is complaining about their people not doing what they said they would, not following through on expectations, not inputting data into the CRM, or making the same mistake over and over again, then that manager needs coaching. My rule of thumb is if you're complaining about your people a lot, there's something you need to do as a manager or leader to change that, not just keep complaining. Another sign is if the sales manager is always rescuing the day, closing the deal themselves, and not allowing their salespeople to fail in order to get better.

### **Q: What mistakes do SMB owners often make when trying to coach their sales teams themselves?**

**A:** They believe they are more effective than they are. An owner has too many hats to wear. We believe we have put the required attention into supporting and developing people, but it's usually shortcutted because we have so much responsibility. The other mistake is they're just focused on the department making some sales for the month. They're not focused on developing people so those people will not only make sales today but will also be growing and getting better over time. It's a good problem to have if you're afraid that your people are going to be recruited somewhere else because they keep getting better.

### **Q: At a high level, if you had to create a Sales Manager Coaching Guide, what would the essential elements be?**

**A:** They would be what's in my book.

* **Belief in People:** Your belief in people is number one. How much you believe in them determines how effective you are as a leader and how fast or slow your people will grow.
* **Clarity in Expectations:** Specifically, job descriptions, sales process, compensation, and CRM data input requirements need to be clarified.
* **Accountable Environment:** If you clarify expectations, you'll be able to have an accountable environment by using a dashboard and reporting metrics to see if people are meeting expectations in activity and results.
* **Engaging and Effective Sales Meetings:** The sole purpose of a sales meeting is to help salespeople sell more. If that is what the meetings are really all about, the salespeople will know it and will want to come because they're not just there to get beat up.
* **Conversations:** Conversations that communicate that you care about someone. Care about them enough to encourage and edify them when they're doing well and care about them enough to challenge and confront them when they're not. And then to be open and honest with your feedback.

### **Q: How should an SMB evaluate if sales management coaching would be beneficial?**

**A:** It would be beneficial if your sales manager is not meeting their quota anymore and they say it's because they're spending too much time working with everyone else. Or, if they are still the top performer, but no one else on the team is improving and they're still saving the day. The SMB needs to decide if they want their sales leader to be more effective and raise the level of sales in the whole department, or if they don't mind if one person sells everything.

### **Q: What's the difference between a sales management coaching program and a sales management training course, and when should an SMB use each?**

**A:** A training course is something you go to, learn a lot of information about what you should be doing, and then you go back and do the best you can. We just don't learn as much over a day or two of training. A coaching program, at least with us, infuses training with coaching. We talk about real-life scenarios. Rather than just pushing information onto you, we're looking at your actual job descriptions and evaluating them. We're coaching them with their real data on how to make it better. We're teaching in the real application and then coaching them to do the work during the week. The following week, we evaluate how they did. This reinforces the learning and helps it stick.

### **Q: How can an SMB measure whether coaching is improving sales performance?**

**A:** Sales go up is one. That's the bottom line. If people are just happier and learning, but they're not selling more, then who cares? At least in sales. Retention is another thing to measure.

### **Q: What is the cost of not investing in sales management coaching?**

**A:** You can lose people that could be producers for you. Good employees who can get better with good leadership or management. They might get okay on their own, but they're not the self-driven, top-gun type. With a good coach, they can be better. The cost of not investing is you're not getting the most out of each salesperson. You're hanging on to the ones you like that aren't performing, or you're losing your best salespeople because they're not appreciated, or other departments can't handle their strong personality, and you need a manager in there to keep the peace.

### **Q: How do you know when to bring in an external coach/consultant vs. upskilling your sales manager internally?**

**A:** It's a question of what's best. When you upskill a seller-manager internally, you're going to take a little bit away from their sales as they learn how to develop others. It's really about how many sales you want from them. If they're losing money because their sales are a little bit muted, then it's not so good, and you'd want to look at bringing in a fractional coach.

### **Your Sales Have Plateaued. The Problem Isn't Your Team, It's the Lack of Coaching.**

If you’re a business owner, you’ve poured everything into getting your company to where it is today. But now, you’re facing a familiar and frustrating challenge: you’ve hit a sales ceiling. The effort, strategies, and even the salespeople that propelled you to this point are no longer enough to achieve higher growth. The engine seems to have stalled.

Many owners believe the solution lies in hiring new salespeople or finding a magic bullet strategy. The real issue, however, is often much closer to home. The problem isn’t a fault of your salespeople; it’s a gap in leadership. Your business has a manager who puts out fires, but it lacks a coach who can build a winning team.

#### **The High Cost of the "Accidental" Sales Manager**

In most small businesses, the owner is the default sales manager. You wear too many hats, and because you were likely the first and best salesperson, sales leadership fell onto your already full plate. While well-intentioned, this approach is often ineffective. You simply don't have the time or specific expertise to focus on developing people for the long term when you have a dozen other responsibilities.

This leads to common symptoms of ineffective management. See if any of these sound familiar:

* **A Culture of Firefighting:** You spend your days running around putting out fires, rescuing deals at the last minute, and closing sales that your team couldn't.
* **Lack of Accountability:** You find yourself constantly complaining that your team isn't following through, not inputting data into the CRM, or making the same mistakes over and over again.
* **No Clear Expectations:** Without clear expectations for performance and activity, there can be no true accountability. The environment becomes one of excuses rather than results.

These issues have tangible costs. You fail to get the most out of each salesperson, you hang on to underperformers you like, and you risk losing your best people because they aren't being challenged or developed. This is the cycle of stagnation that keeps your business from scaling.

#### **The Real Difference: Sales Coaching vs. Sales Training**

Many people use the terms "training" and "coaching" interchangeably, but they are fundamentally different. Understanding this difference is the first step to building a better sales team.

* **Sales Training is an event.** It’s a course, a video series, or a book study where information is taught.
* **Sales Coaching is a process.** It’s the ongoing work of reinforcing what was learned in training and applying it to real-life scenarios.

Think of professional sports. Every player has been trained in the fundamentals, but they also have a head coach and position coaches to help them improve continuously. A sales manager’s coaching is uniquely impactful because it’s holistic. As a manager, you see the total picture—you have the performance metrics, you understand each person’s motivations, and you know how to push them to win. The best managers know that coaching is what makes training stick.

#### **The 5 Pillars of a Winning Sales Coaching Playbook**

Effective sales management isn’t a mystery; it’s a discipline built on a clear set of fundamentals. If you want to transform your sales department, you must build a culture of coaching centered on these five essential pillars.

1. **Start with Belief in People:** Your belief in your team is the foundation. How much you believe in them directly determines how effective you are as a leader and how quickly your people will grow. If you don't believe in someone, you must either fix your perspective or fix your team.
2. **Provide Crystal-Clear Expectations:** You cannot hold people accountable for standards you haven’t set. Expectations must be clarified in four key areas: their job description, the company’s official sales process, their compensation plan, and the requirements for CRM data input.
3. **Build a Culture of Accountability:** Once expectations are clear, you can create an accountable environment. Using dashboards and reporting metrics, you can objectively measure whether people are meeting expectations in both their activity levels and their results.
4. **Run Sales Meetings That Actually Help People Sell:** The sole purpose of a sales meeting is to help salespeople sell more. If your meetings are just a session to beat up the team on their numbers, they will dread coming. When meetings are focused on strategy, skill-building, and collaborative problem-solving, salespeople will see them as valuable and will want to attend.
5. **Master Conversations That Build, Not Break:** Great coaching requires conversations that communicate you care. You must care enough to encourage and edify your salespeople when they are doing well, and care enough to challenge and confront them when they are not. Open, honest, and direct feedback is crucial for growth.

#### **How to Measure the ROI of Effective Coaching**

Investing in coaching isn't just about making people feel good; it's about driving business results. The return on your investment can be measured in two critical ways.

The most obvious metric is that **sales go up.** If your team is learning and morale is high, but they aren’t selling more, the coaching isn’t working. The bottom line is the bottom line.

The second, often overlooked, metric is **employee retention.** The cost of not investing in coaching is that you lose good employees who could become top producers with the right leadership. A good coach can develop the "B" players into "A" players and create an environment where your top performers want to stay.

#### **Is It Time to Bring in a Coach?**

If you're wondering whether your current approach is working, ask yourself these questions:

* Are you (or your sales manager) still the top performer while the rest of the team stagnates?
* Is your manager complaining that they can't hit their own quota because they’re spending too much time working with everyone else?
* Are you avoiding promoting a top seller into management because you can't afford to lose their sales?

If you answered yes, it may be time to consider a dedicated coaching program. A one-day training course can be informative, but it rarely leads to lasting change. A true coaching program is different—it infuses training with real-world application. A coach works with your team on their actual deals, refines their processes in real time, and reinforces learning week after week until it becomes second nature.

#### **Conclusion: Stop Managing and Start Coaching for Growth**

To break through your sales plateau, you must shift your focus from short-term, reactive management to long-term, proactive coaching. By implementing a structured coaching playbook, you can unlock the full potential of your sales team, build a scalable engine for growth, and finally free yourself up to focus on leading the entire business forward.
